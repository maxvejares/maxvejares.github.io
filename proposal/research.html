State Capacity

Taming Nature, Building States

Book project. Chile, Argentina, Brazil, United States.

Local ecologies shaped the origins of bureaucratic capacity in the Americas. Ruling coalitions seeking to exploit commodities for world markets between roughly 1870 and 1930 confronted environments that imposed sharply different demands on the state. Where production required heavy transformation of the landscape, as in Chile's Araucanía forests and the United States' Great Plains, the state deployed military, technocratic, and bureaucratic resources at scale, and bureaucratic capacity grew with it. Where commodity production bypassed the state, or where haciendas operated as micro-sovereignties that blocked state penetration, bureaucratic development stagnated. The book combines archival research, comparative-historical analysis, and GIS mapping of original subnational administrative data to trace these mechanisms across four countries.

Varieties of State-Building: Ecology, Clientelism, and Bureaucratic Rule in Chile

Perspectives on Politics, 2025.

Canonical theories of state-building assume a perpetual appetite for extraction and standardization. But subnational regions present different appeals and challenges to ruling coalitions. States extend bureaucratic rule over peripheries with valuable assets and preserve patrimonial bastions where local elites deliver clientelistic support, producing wide subnational heterogeneity in the reach of the state. Using original data from Chilean censuses, budgets, and other primary sources, the paper shows that Chile's ruling coalition modernized its peripheries while deepening their traditionalism.

Democracy

Manorialism as Insurance: Landholding, Democracy, and State Capture in Chile

With Sebastian Cortesi.

When do economic elites support democracy, and how do they protect their assets against its redistributive effects? Contrary to the conventional wisdom that landed elites resist competitive politics, this paper argues that landlords may actively support franchise expansion when agrarian structures provide sufficient electoral control. Under manorialism—characterized by self-sufficient estates and strong clientelistic bonds between lords and peasants—landowners leverage their patron-client ties to mobilize and oversee peasant voters, effectively turning enfranchisement into political insurance. Using an original panel dataset of Chilean departments (1862–1920) and a difference-in-differences design, we show that the franchise expanded significantly more in hacienda-heavy departments after the 1874 electoral reform. Rather than blocking state penetration, landlords colonized local political institutions, a pattern that persisted until the introduction of the secret ballot in 1958.

Industrial Policy

State Capacity and Industrial Policy

The conventional wisdom holds that a minimum threshold of state capacity is a precondition for successful industrial policy. The historical record suggests a reversal of that causal arrow: large-scale developmental projects often build state capacity as a necessary byproduct of carrying them out. Railroads, electrification, mineral concessions, state-owned enterprises, and large infrastructure works force the state to develop fiscal, planning, and technical capabilities that did not exist beforehand. This paper argues that capacity and industrial policy are mutually constitutive rather than sequential, and develops the argument through a set of cases in which developmental projects were the proximate cause of bureaucratic formation rather than its consequence. Geopolitics and the Waves of Industrial Policy

Five waves of industrial policy, from the first globalization through the current green industrial era, have been shaped less by what works in the abstract than by the geopolitical settlements that governed each period. The paper develops this argument through a comparative-historical account that contrasts Latin America's downstream trap under import substitution with East Asia's upstream-first heavy and chemical industrialization, and then carries the comparison into the current race for clean-energy manufacturing. Upstream targeting (capital goods, heavy industry) generates stronger backward linkages and technological learning than downstream targeting, but requires higher state capacity to execute, because upstream sectors face longer payoff horizons and greater rent-seeking pressures.
